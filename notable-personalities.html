Luis Mangalus Taruc (pronounced as [ˈluwis ˈtɐɾuk]; June 21, 1913 – May 4, 2005) was a prominent Filipino political figure and insurgent during the agricultural unrest spanning from the 1930s until the conclusion of the Cold War era. He served as the head of the Hukbalahap group (derived from Hukbong Bayan Laban sa Hapon) from 1942 to 1950. His association with the movement stemmed from his exposure to the challenges faced by Filipino farmers during his student years in the early 1930s. Throughout World War II, Taruc led the Hukbalahap in guerrilla activities against the Japanese occupants in the Philippines.

Influenced by the socialist ideals of Pedro Abad Santos from San Fernando and inspired by earlier Katipunan revolutionaries like Felipe Salvador, Taruc became part of the Aguman ding Maldang Tala-pagobra (AMT, Kapampangan for 'Union of Peasant Workers') and, in 1938, joined the Partido Sosyalista ng Pilipinas (Socialist Party of the Philippines). Later on, the party merged with the Partido Komunista ng Pilipinas under the Common Front strategy, and Taruc assumed leadership as the Commander-in-Chief of the military wing aimed at combating the Japanese occupation.

Following the conflict with Japan, the Hukbalahap persisted in advocating for agrarian reform. Despite Taruc and seven colleagues being elected to the House of Representatives, the government under Manuel Roxas prevented them from assuming their seats in Congress. The Taruc faction opposed the parity rights demanded by the U.S. from post-independence Philippines for rehabilitation funding. Over the subsequent five years, Taruc shifted away from parliamentary efforts and once again resorted to armed struggle. At its peak, the Hukbalahap amassed a fighting force estimated between 10,000 to 30,000. In 2017, the National Historical Commission of the Philippines honored Taruc as a hero for his dedication as a "nationalist and advocate for the rights of farmers and laborers."
